What Is Brain Tumor?

A brain tumor is an abnormal growth of cells within or around brain tissues. Tumors may be benign or malignant and can affect cognition, vision, movement, memory, and hormonal control depending on location and growth pattern. Early detection supports timely treatment planning.

Common Causes

Most tumors have multifactorial causes that may include genetic mutations, inherited syndromes, prior radiation exposure, and environmental risk factors. In many patients, no single identifiable cause is confirmed, which is why screening awareness matters.

Warning Signs

Persistent headaches, vomiting, seizures, visual disturbances, imbalance, personality changes, and progressive weakness can indicate neurological concern. Symptoms vary by tumor size and region; new persistent symptoms should be medically evaluated.

Prevention Tips

No guaranteed prevention exists, but risk reduction includes avoiding unnecessary radiation exposure, maintaining healthy lifestyle habits, controlling chronic conditions, and attending regular medical checkups when risk history is present.

Classification

Glioma

Gliomas originate from glial cells and can range from low-grade to high-grade forms. Depending on subtype and grade, treatment may include surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or multimodal management.

Classification

Meningioma

Meningiomas arise from meningeal tissue covering the brain and spinal cord. Many are slow-growing and benign, but size and location can still produce substantial neurological symptoms requiring intervention.

Classification

Pituitary Tumor

Pituitary tumors develop near the pituitary gland and may alter hormone production. Clinical effects can include headache, visual field deficits, and endocrine imbalance requiring focused evaluation.
